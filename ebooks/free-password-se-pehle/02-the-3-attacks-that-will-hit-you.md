# Chapter 2: The 3 Attacks That Will Hit You

---

You don't need to worry about every type of cyberattack in the world. Most of them will never affect you. But three of them almost certainly will — if they haven't already.

These aren't exotic hacker techniques from movies. These are everyday attacks that target ordinary people, and they work because almost nobody knows how to spot them.

After this chapter, you will.

## Attack #1: Phishing (The Fake Message)

Phishing is the single most common cyberattack on the planet. It's responsible for over **90% of all data breaches**. And it doesn't require any technical skill at all from the attacker.

Here's how it works:

You receive a message — an email, SMS, or WhatsApp message — that looks like it's from someone you trust. Your bank. Amazon. IRCTC. Instagram. It says something urgent:

> *"Your account has been suspended. Verify your identity now to avoid permanent ban."*

> *"₹49,999 debited from your account. If this wasn't you, click here immediately."*

> *"You've won a ₹10,000 cashback. Claim it before it expires."*

The message includes a link. You click it. You land on a page that looks **exactly** like the real website. You enter your login details. And just like that — the attacker has your username and password.

### How to Spot Phishing in 5 Seconds

**Step 1: Check the sender.**
- Real: `noreply@instagram.com`
- Fake: `noreply@instagram-security-verify.com`

**Step 2: Hover over (or long-press) the link before clicking.**
- Real: `https://instagram.com/accounts/login`
- Fake: `https://insta-login.verify-account.xyz/login`

**Step 3: Look for pressure.**
Legitimate companies never say "Act now or lose your account." That urgency is the attacker's most powerful weapon.

**The Rule:** If a message makes you feel urgency, fear, or excitement — stop. Go directly to the official website or app yourself. Never click the link in the message.

## Attack #2: Public Wi-Fi Traps

You're at a coffee shop, airport, or hotel. You see free Wi-Fi. You connect. You check your email, scroll Instagram, maybe even log into your bank.

What you don't know: someone else on that same network can see **everything you're doing**.

### How it works

There are two common versions:

**Version 1: The Evil Twin**
An attacker creates a Wi-Fi network with the same name as the real one. You connect to "Starbucks_Free_WiFi" — but it's not Starbucks. It's a laptop sitting two tables away, routing all your traffic through it.

**Version 2: The Sniffer**
On an open (unencrypted) Wi-Fi network, an attacker runs a simple software tool that captures all unencrypted traffic. Every website you visit, every form you fill out, every password you type on an HTTP page.

### How to Protect Yourself

1. **Never access banking or sensitive accounts on public Wi-Fi.** Wait until you're on mobile data or your home network.
2. **Check for HTTPS.** Look for the lock icon in your browser's address bar. If it says "Not Secure," don't enter anything.
3. **Use mobile data for sensitive tasks.** Your 4G/5G connection is encrypted and far safer than public Wi-Fi.
4. **Forget the network after use.** Go to your Wi-Fi settings and remove saved public networks so your phone doesn't auto-connect later.

**The Rule:** Treat every public Wi-Fi network as if someone is watching. Because they might be.

## Attack #3: SIM Swap Fraud

This one is terrifying because it bypasses almost every security measure most people use.

Here's the scenario: An attacker calls your mobile carrier (Airtel, Jio, Vi) and convinces them to transfer your phone number to a new SIM card — one that the attacker controls. Once they have your number, they receive all your calls and SMS messages, including **OTPs (One-Time Passwords)**.

With your OTPs, they can:
- Reset your email password
- Log into your bank account
- Access your UPI apps
- Take over your social media

### How SIM Swap Attacks Happen

1. **Social engineering:** The attacker calls customer care pretending to be you. They might have your name, address, and Aadhaar number from a previous data leak.
2. **Insider job:** In some cases, a corrupt employee at the telecom company processes the swap.
3. **Phishing first:** The attacker first phishes your details (email, DOB, address), then uses them to answer the carrier's verification questions.

### How to Protect Yourself

1. **Never use SMS for 2FA.** Use an authenticator app instead (we'll set this up in Chapter 3).
2. **Set a SIM lock PIN** on your phone — a separate PIN that's required to use the SIM card.
3. **Register on your carrier's security portal.** Most Indian carriers (Jio, Airtel) allow you to set additional verification for SIM swaps.
4. **Watch for warning signs:** If your phone suddenly shows "No Signal" for no reason, call your carrier immediately. A SIM swap might be in progress.

**The Rule:** Your phone number is not a security tool. It's a target. Stop using it as your primary verification method.

## The Pattern

Notice something about all three attacks?

None of them require "hacking" in the technical sense. There's no one breaking into a server room or typing green code on a black screen. Every one of these attacks works because of **human behavior** — clicking a link, trusting a Wi-Fi name, relying on SMS codes.

That's actually good news. Because it means **you don't need technology to defend yourself. You just need awareness.**
